A high quality full motion video game in a modern setting with moral weight. If it was the last day of your life, what would you do?

Seize the Day will be the culmination of my vision for a high quality Full Motion Video Game designed for the iPad. The game will feature around two hours of HD video branched out into approximately 30 minutes per full playthrough. The actual length of each playthrough will vary with some paths ending sooner. The player will be able to significantly impact the outcome of the game through interaction with the video.Â 

Gameplay will largely consist of single touch interactions with various twists. A player profile will be developed in the beginning of the game based on choices that are made. This profile will drive different players to get different results from the exact same touch interaction. Timing, the location touched or a choice between two objects to touch (or not touch) could also all drive diverging storylines. The use of single touch alone makes it possible for anyone to pick this game and enjoy it regardless of prior gaming experience. Players can also choose to not touch anything at all and see how the story unfolds like they're watching a movie.

The combined rise of iOS gaming and independent film-making over the last few years has made it possible to realize this vision now. There is an amazing level of independent film-making talent available today to tap into for this project. Kickstarter and other funding platforms are full of great directors with proven track records of delivering quality films at very modest budgets. The work they are doing blows away the million dollar production budgets of FMV games of the past. The intimate feel of iOS gaming will also heighten the experience of really feeling like youâ€™re driving the story.

The story will be a modern day drama based on a day in the life of a single man. He wakes after a very vivid death dream wondering if it was a sign that he is destined to die today. As the day progresses the player (you) will decide how to make the most of his day. With the possible threat of it being your last day on earth will you do? Will you try to have as much fun as possible or will you try to make a difference in the world? This story is told through three ten minute acts.Â 

Act one is an introduction to the story and allows the player to develop a personal profile through their choices. Actions in this section will impact how other people see you through the rest of the story. As you wake up you face a series of choices that will define the person you are. The clothes you wear, the breakfast you eat and how you spend you free time in the morning will define you.

Act two establishes a set of choices to make on how your day will progress. A series of events will unfold that will influence your final destination. The splash image seen above is a representation from a scene where you're traveling from your home to meet a friend for lunch. Will you take the quick route and drive, saving yourself some time, or will you slow down and take the long walk there? How will this one decision impact the rest of your day?Â 

Act three will branch out into a selection of possible endings that are wildly different from each other. There will be three main branches for players to go through with variations in each of those branches. The end result will be over a dozen unique endings. Â 

I am not a programmer by trade, so I didn't start this process thinking I would be creating this game myself. I thought I would do a little investigation into the possibility or realizing my vision and then look to outsource the programming. The only problem I had was that there weren't a lot of resources on how to make an FMV game work on iOS. As I looked deeper into the technical side money started to get tight at home and I wondered if I would have the resources to pay for development of a prototype. I wasn't sure how I was going to progress.

I decided to look into doing to programming myself. I had some history with VBA programming, so I didn't have to start from scratch. I started out reading a book on Objective-C and then moved onto a book on iOS programming. After that I was ready to begin trying to build the basic portions of the game. I needed a menu, the ability to play video, the ability to recognize whether objects in videos were touched and then change videos based on those choices. After a month of long nights I finally had a very rough prototype.

I began showing this prototype around to people and it felt like a disaster. People were impressed that I was able to build the framework, but they ran into issues actually trying to play the "game". I was embarrassed by the product I was presenting. After another month of long nights I had polished the "game" to the point where I could take pride in the quality of the product I had created.

I had solved all of the initial challenges with the prototype. A tutorial was created to walk through how the game worked. A visual queue was added to let the player know when a scene was interactive. Another visual queue was added to let the player know when a touch interaction was registered. A pause menu was created. All of the bugs had been fixed and the video trimmed to present a better experience.

This gave me the confidence I needed to begin asking for support. I was now confident I could deliver on the promise of delivering a finished game. Here is a walkthrough of that prototype running on an iPad Mini:Â 

Initially I wanted to have a director lined up and ready to go before starting the Kickstarter campaign. The director is really important because a large part of this project will depend on the quality of the video. Without a director I am also unable to produce a real trailer or teaser for Seize the Day. After putting a lot of thought into this decision, I decided to wait to secure a director until funding was completed. Waiting until the completion of the Kickstarter campaign has a number of benefits:

  • Publicity from the Kickstarter campaign may attract interest from new directors
  • Having an exact timeline and funding in place makes it significantly easier for a director to commit to the project
  • I can build all of the Kickstarter rewards into the director's contract
  • Supporter feedback can be incorporated into the decision
  • I'll be in a stronger bargaining position

I am absolutely certain that waiting to select a director will lead to a better product at the end of this project. In order to mitigate the impact if not having a specific director attached to this project, I reviewed the budget I had originally outlined and removed any expenses that were not absolutely necessary to the development of this game.

Please feel free to reach out to me if you or someone you know would be interesting in directing this project. While I want to reserve the final contract until after the campaign is completed, it would be really beneficial to have initial discussions prior to that.Â

The story of Seize the Day began a year and a half ago when I was inspired by my brother in law's success with indie films. At the same time I was also watching a booming iOS platform that was doubling in size every year. This confluence made me see the possibility to revitalize Full Motion Video Games on iOS devices. I knew that with a rising indie film-making scene it would be possible to deliver superior quality to those old games at a fraction of the cost. The biggest challenge I saw would be to secure the funding to get started.

In order to help secure funding thought I needed a quick hook. I started looking into licenses from old Sega CD games. Of course the first one I thought of was Night Trap. I spent an entire weekend investigating the fate of that license before I ended up at Hasbro as the final resting place. I reached out to multiple contacts in their licensing department and did not get a single reply. At that time (pre-Double Fine Adventure) I didn't think funding would be possible without a strong license. I regretted the wasted weekend, so I gave up on the idea at that time.

The story picks back up in March 2012, when it was the year end review process at work. I had a long track record of above average performance and I had just come off a year where I had worked harder than ever. Throughout the year I was staying late regularly, working holidays and weekends and generally going above and beyond in my work. When my review came in at average performance I was extremely upset at the time I had invested in work. That review was an indication that all of those extra hours were wasted. I vowed that would never happen again.

Looking ahead to my goals for 2012, I tried to figure out where I could best invest the extra 20 hours a week I had been working. After looking for alternatives I decided that trying to make my own game would be the best use of my resources. I would get new experience in a multitude of areas in the gaming business and have a tangible output from that experience. It was a win/win solution. I already had an idea ready to go and the growing success of Kickstarter campaigns allowed me to forget about getting an existing property.Â 

The company Fumovi is just one person, so this is really all about me: Josh Kanownik. I am a lifelong gamer that currently works in the video game industry for a major publisher in the sales department. I am extremely passionate about playing games and championing the value they provide to society.Â 

One day I hope to run a major publisher and my work on this game is a part of my plan to accomplish that goal. I strongly believe in the publisher model for video games and I believe passionate and proactive management on the publisher side can result in better games for everyone (I get the irony here, but it would be a breach of fiduciary duty for a major publisher to invest in this game at this point). I hope to build a future where I can help deliver the highest possible quality of games to a larger audience than ever before.Â

Risks and challenges Learn about accountability on Kickstarter

The biggest challenge is going to be creating a game that is really fun to play. The prototype is interesting to look at and fully functioning, but it is not really a game. To help with this challenge I'll be able working with paper decision trees to iterate on the design as I go along. I'll be playing through the game repeatedly before the script is finalized and video production begins.The formalized QA process should also highlight issues that can be worked on prior to the game launching.

There is a risk that I'll be unable to secure a director that can deliver the level of quality I'm hoping to get. This could impact the timeline and result in delays. In an extreme case I may be unable to find a director at all. If this happens I may have to perform this duty myself or split duties into multiple roles with myself taking a more direct producing role.

There is a risk that the mix in Kickstarter rewards will be unfavorable and costs will be higher than expected. I have fully costed all of the rewards so I am confident that all of the rewards can be delivered as promised, but I ran through a sampling of potential backer numbers for the different rewards and it is possible that the cost will exceed $4,000 I have budgeted. I will personally fund any overages in reward costs.

As a one man production crew, there is a risk that something would happen to me that would prevent this game from coming out. A serious illness to myself or anyone in my family would result in delays to this project. There are other catastrophic events that could occur up to and including my death which would impact the project.

FAQ

  • Is the game going to look like your promo video?

    No. Absolutely not. The game will be shot by an experienced director using professional equipment. It will look like a real movie. I don't have that level of equipment myself, which is why parts of the promo video do not look that good.

    If you're not familiar with the level of quality that can be done for the ~$7,500 I have budgeted, I encourage you to look around film & video projects on Kickstarter. I think you'll be pleasantly surprised. You may find something else you want to support too.

    As you're looking at projects keep in mind that a modern day drama significantly reduces the costs of production. We won't have to worry about special effects or building sets. We'll also be shooting similar scenes back to back which will further reduce costs.

    Last updated: Sat, Feb 2 2013 3:34 am EST
  • Is this really a game?

    Yes! It is most definitely a game. I just don't know how far it will go towards a more traditional game yet. I may have to sacrifice certain gameplay elements to maintain the integrity of the story I want to tell. At the very least there is an objective (Seize the Day), the profile system will be stat based behind the scenes and timing/location of touches will come into play. This will make Seize the Day far more than just a video-based choose your own adventure.

    I will be testing other elements such as hidden objects in the video and scoring. I'm not sure if I can come up with a scoring system for morality, but I am going to try. It is going to be really interesting to iterate through that system. As I flesh out the story I may have other ideas for how I can incorporate additional gameplay into game.

    Last updated: Sun, Feb 3 2013 3:47 am EST
  • Do you really expect me to believe that you're going to make the greatest FMV game ever?

    No, you should be skeptical. You should be really skeptical. I'm just hoping that you're also really intrigued by the possibility. I'm betting a lot of my money, my time and my reputation on my ability to deliver a quality game. If I deliver a bad game it could kill my career potential in the gaming industry.

    I wouldn't be doing this if I thought there was a chance I wouldn't deliver a good game. Setting the bar at the greatest FMV game ever gives me a goal to reach for. If I fall short a little short of that goal I'll still end up with a good game.

    Last updated: Sat, Feb 2 2013 12:13 am EST
  • What about the writing? Are you a writer? The writing is going to be pretty important.

    Nope. This is a tough question to answer. I can't really prove that I'm a good writer and I don't want to spoil the story with what I've written already. You're taking a risk that the writing won't be good.

    One benefit that the story will have is that it will be a collaborative experience between myself and the director. There will be plenty of opportunities for them to step in and improve the writing as we work through the production. I'm also open to everyone involved in the production (including backers at the producer levels) offering feedback and suggested changes for the script.

    Last updated: Sat, Feb 2 2013 12:13 am EST

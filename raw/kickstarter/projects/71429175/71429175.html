K-2 students can use this app to practice the decoding skills they need to read. Help teachers get this app into their classrooms!

What Are Nonsense Words?

Nonsense words are two and three letter words that follow the spelling pattern of Vowel-Consonant or Consonant-Vowel-Consonant, such as 'ip' and 'lon'. They use the sounds most commonly associated for each letter, so they are easy for a beginning reader to decode. These same spelling patters form the basis of many regular words that beginning readers will encounter such as 'it' and 'lot'.

Why Do You Need an App for That?

You don't need an app, but it certainly helps. Beginning readers need as much decoding practice as possible because learning to read is hard and involves many skills. When teachers teach children how to read, many often use nonsense words. Top reading researchers report that nonsense words are a great way to practice the skills needed to decode words (see list near the bottom of the page). When reading simple words (whether they are regular or nonsense), a child needs to:

  • know the sound each letter makes
  • blend these sounds together 
  • say the word quickly and correctly
A child learns this through a lot of practice. But practice doesn't make perfect- practice makes permanent. A child needs an immediate response to tell her if she made the right sound for each letter or word. This is often where a teacher, parent, or older sibling/friend helps when practicing. But people's time is limited and this practice often doesn't happen as much as it could.
Research from Good, Simmons & Smith (1998) has shown that if children don't master these basic skills when they are in grades K-2, it will lead them in a trajectory of academic struggling (the red line in the graph). Students will have a much better chance at succeeding in school (the green line) if there is extra practice in the early grades when the gap between the lines in the graph is smaller.

That's where this app comes into play. The Nonsense Words app provides an opportunity for children to practice the decoding skills they have learned in class. Because it's an app, children can play anywhere, at any time- increasing their reading ability in what would otherwise be lost learning moments. Because it's in a game format, children will more likely spend more time engaged and playing it- thus practicing these skills and becoming better readers.

Will This App Really Help Children Read?

Simply put- yes it will. Nonsense Words is built upon the foundation of 30 years of research and a reading assessment system used by tens of thousands of teachers across the country.

A Research Based Approach   
DIBELS® (Dynamic Indicators of Basic Early Literacy Skills) is a series of researched based assessments that teachers have been using with students to track their reading progress for decades. One of the DIBELS® assessments, Nonsense Word Fluency (NWF), measures how many words and letter sounds students in grades K-2 correctly identify in one minute. Other assessments focus on the first sound of a word (First Sound Fluency), the sound each letter makes in a simple word (Phoneme Segmentation Fluency), and more.

How the App Works 
The blueprint of the app is described below and details its functionality. When the app is built, its graphics and overall design will be more child friendly. When playing Nonsense Words, readers can choose to play one of three games and select one of four players. There will be over 500 nonsense words used in the app.

Timed Game: A reader is shown a nonsense word such as ‘lut’ and then reads the word out loud or says each letter’s sound, depending on her reading ability. Then she checks the actual sound of each letter or word by tapping the green bar or buttons below it. If she read the word or made the right sounds, she taps a star to advance to another word and earns a point. If she missed, then she taps the advance button to show the next word. After one minute, the game ends and the score is shown.

There are two practice games, with no time limits or scoring. These games address common reading errors either in the part of a word or with a specific letter.

Beginning/ Middle/ End: The reader selects the beginning, middle, or end in which the same random letter is used for five consecutive words (i.e., lem, laz, lig, lod, lus, jac, jun, jep, jil, jom). This provides support for readers to decode the beginning, medial, or final sounds in words.  
Select a Letter: The reader selects a letter that will be used in all of the words (i.e., ta, wot, teb, fut, tod). This provides readers the opportunity to practice decoding a specific letter in many words and in different placements.

For Early Literacy Educators: A Detailed Explanation

If you are a K-2 teacher or literacy coach/expert, this section is for you. If you’re not, you can skip ahead to the next section.

The DIBELS® Nonsense Word Fluency (NWF) assessment determines if students understand the alphabetic principle and basic phonics through scores of the Correct Letter Sounds (CLS) and Whole Words Read (WWR). The goal is for students to read nonsense words as whole words rather than segmenting them into their letter sounds. The ‘Nonsense Words!’ app allows students to listen to both the letter sounds as well as the whole word to align with this DIBELS® assessment. In the Timed Game, students can score themselves so they can track their progress as they increase their fluency. Please note, these self-scores from the app should not be compared to actual DIBELS® scores. Only a trained teacher with the appropriate DIBELS® test materials can administer it to a student to determine accurate assessment scores.

If a student exhibits a common reading error such as problems with beginning, medial, or ending sounds or with a specific letter, the ‘Nonsense Words!’ app has two games in which students can practice those specific skills. Once the student has increased proficiency in that reading error, she can see if she can increase her score in the Timed Game.

What Are Your Plans if This is Funded?

An experienced Apple (iOS) app developer has already reviewed the blueprint of the app and is able to create it once this Kickstarter project is funded.  

An agile methodology is being used to develop this app. This version of Nonsense Words app is what's called the minimum viable product. That means it has all of the necessary functions to work but nothing more. Future sales of the app will fund ongoing development. If there is an abundance of funding beyond the Kickstarter project goal, there are further development plans. These include improving the sound and graphics, adding speech recognition (so the app can hear if the child said the right word), and developing the app for the Android platform.

As part of this Kickstarter project, a free version of Nonsense Words will also be created so any child can practice their reading skills on any iPad, iPhone, or iPod touch. The free version will include the three games, a subset of the nonsense words, and several choices for the practice games.

How Did You Get This Idea?

As an education consultant, I have spent three years helping Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) educators use cutting edge tools to assess beginning readers. Within this second largest school district in the country, I've trained and worked with over 1,000 teachers in over 100 schools to use DIBELS® based assessments and reading interventions. Just over a year ago, many of these teachers began asking me if I knew of apps that students could use to practice the skills assessed by DIBELS®. After some investigating, I found very few, and none that were based in research and targeted these specific reading skills. So out of necessity came an opportunity... and this project was born.

The Research

Listed below are some recent research findings on the use of nonsense words to help students read.

Fien, H., Park, Y., Baker, S. K., Smith, J. L. M., Stoolmiller, M., & Kame'enui, E. J. (2010). An examination of the relation of nonsense word fluency initial status and gains to reading outcomes for beginning readers. School Psychology Review, 39, 631I653.  

Fuchs, L. S., Fuchs, D., & Compton, D. L. (2004). Monitoring early reading development in first grade: Word identification fluency versus nonsense word fluency. Exceptional Children, 71(1), 7-21.  

Good, R. H., III, Baker, S. K., & Peyton, J. A. (2009). Making sense of Nonsense Word Fluency: Determining adequate progress in early first-grade reading. Reading & Writing Quarterly, 25(1), 33-56.  

Good, R. H., Simmons, D. C., & Smith, S. B. (1998). Effective academic interventions in the United States: Evaluating and enhancing the acquisition of early reading skills. School Psychology Review, 27, 740-753. [Joint publication with Educational and Child Psychology.]  

Ritchey, K. D. (2008). Assessing letter sound knowledge: A comparison of Letter Sound Fluency and Nonsense Word Fluency. Exceptional Children, 74(4), 487-506.  

Vanderwood, M. L., Linklater, D., & Healy, K. (2008). Predictive accuracy of nonsense word fluency for English language learners. School Psychology Review, 37(1), 5-17.

Risks and challenges Learn about accountability on Kickstarter

I’m a co-founder of an educational technology consulting company called 2it Education Solutions, that has developed, managed, and launched many new educational technology projects over the past 13+ years. As with any new project, experience and planning can mitigate much of the risks. I've leveraged our education and production experience to create the content and blueprint for the app. In addition, I've contacted an experienced app developer whom I've worked with on a project before who has assured us that he is able to create the app in a timely manner.

The outstanding risks involve production delays and over-runs due to unforeseen technical issues and scheduling problems. The former does not pose a significant risk since the app does not involve complicated coding. The latter is not a major concern since the target date is self-imposed and can be extended a few weeks with no harmful impact.

A marketing challenge is that the community of supporters of this project is small. We hope that the Kickstarter community can help spread the word to teachers, parents of young children, and anyone who supports teachers and young readers.

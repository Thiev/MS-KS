Aleph is an RPG for the PC platform. It is greatly inspired by RPGs of yore, with dungeons full of monsters, traps, and treasure!

Before getting to the meat and potatoes, I would just like to thank you for taking a moment and looking at this project.

Do you remember your favorite games growing up?

I grew up in the 80's and 90's and some of my favorite games of all times come from this time period. Aleph is an, albeit technologically superior, homage to the great games of our past. It is inspired by some of the greatest RPGs of the last three decades, ranging from Final Fantasy, Ultima, Dragon Quest, to Baldur's Gate. However, it is also rather 'modernized', utilizing a robust 2D sprite engine which supports high-quality graphics and music. Don't let the video fool you, most of those cartoony graphics will be done away with. Here is a sample of what is being developed (check the Updates section for more recent examples):

I hope that this combination of old and new brings back fond nostalgic memories to seasoned gamers, as well as offer new excitement and adventure to all.

What is Aleph?

The world of Aleph is vast and brimming with towns, dungeons, monsters, treasures, and more nooks and crannies than you could ever shake a stick+1 at.

It is a large open world, meaning everything is to scale and persistent. There is a day/night cycle which can effect a number of game elements, as well as a weather system. Townsfolk do not exist just to spout one-liners and are highly interactive. In fact, the whole world of Alpeh is interactive. If something is not nailed to the floor, you can pick it up and bring it wherever you wish. This offers a large myriad of opportunities for you to create housing, bases, storage areas for your equipment, and so forth as you see fit. Although you do get an official housing area, there is nothing stopping you from taking any number of locations in the game as your base of operations. Libraries are overflowing with books, many of which offer relevant or helpful information to the player who can utilize it right. Aleph is a living, breathing world, but only if you can protect it from the scores of hideous creatures out to destroy it!

The world is crawling with all manners of bad guys that are intent on ruining your day (and the world!) Fighting these monsters is an important aspect of the game. Battle takes place in a side-view or front-view manner depending upon the direction the monsters are facing. Combat goes at the speed you want it to, it can be fast and fierce, or it can be slow and strategic. Every monster has its own set of strengths and weaknesses, and you are given a vast array of weapons, armour, skills, spells, and party members to overcome your foes.

Although monsters scurry all over the world, they primarily live in the deep, dark crevices of the many dungeons that dot the land. These dungeons are truly a test of skill and ability. Devious traps, mind-bending riddles and puzzles, horrendous monsters, and glorious treasures await within these maws of darkness and death!

In addition to finding ancient treasures and powerful relics, there is a rather extensive crafting system which allows you to create your own weapons, armour, potions, food, provisions, and other items. Weapons and armour weaken over time and will break entirely if not taken care of properly, which adds another layer to the crafting system.

There is an over-arching plot for the game, but it is entirely up to you on how you follow it. There is no set order in which to do things. You are free to roam and explore the vast land masses of Aleph as you see fit. Gather allies if you wish, or attempt to fight solo. Hire mercenaries, destroy villages, save villages, even make diabolical deals with the great evil forces of the world. But beware, your actions carry great consequences. Your moral choices carry great weight with them.

Know what else made old games great?

Books! One of the best experiences when buying a new game was thumbing through the books that came with them. For most old games, this was an instruction manual, but it contained information about the game world, items, enemies, and so forth. For a smaller portion of games, there was a larger companion book that offered great insight into the game world, and was usually written from the perspective of one of the in-game characters (the Ultima games are widely known for this).

Aleph will have a companion book. The book will be from the point of view of a character within the world of Aleph, and it will give a great deal of useful information that the player can utilize to find secrets, overcome traps, solve riddles, and find monster weaknesses. This will be a real, physical book to hold, to feel, to bend the pages or write in.

For people not wanting a physical copy of the book, it will also be available as a PDF, and will be included with the game regardless of medium.

What is the money for?

This game is already about a year into development. I have managed to keep all costs very low by doing a vast majority of the work myself. I did hire on a brilliant and talented composer, and he definitely deserves to be paid more than what I can offer him from my own pockets.

So money will be utilized thusly:

Payment for musical compositions and finishing the soundtrack. This game features all original music.

Hiring on an artist. Although I am content with the current art, there is a lot of licensed art in there, and instead I would like to have more 'original' artwork. This would give the game a more distinct look and feel.

Book production. The physical copy of the book is probably the most costly aspect of this project.

If the amount of money goes over the minimum limit, then I will use it to work on porting the game to other platforms. It would be wonderful to bring this game to the tablet market.

  • What is in the Development Binder for the $250 reward?

There is a lot of in-game background information, character stats and notes, development checklists, tons of scribbles and 'notes to self', lists of weapons, armor, spells, skills, monsters, status ailments, items, world maps, information on cities and dungeons, and vast amounts of various other scraps of information that I used to make the game. There are some alternate spells and skills that did not make it in, and various other systems and features which I didn't use. Although I didn't count the pages, I estimate roughly 200-300 pages fill the binder.

  • What are the restrictions to backer-created rewards (NPCs, Items, Characters, etc)?

    I will try to make the limits as least constricting as possible in order to foster creativity and uniqueness. However, due to certain factors such as the engine, coding, art resources, and so forth, some limits will apply, but I will work with backers to make sure what they want comes to life. Here is some general guides for creating items, npcs, characters, and so forth:

    *Weapons:
    -Weapon types: Dagger, 1-H Sword, 2-H Sword, Axe, Polearm, 1-H Mace, 2-H Mace, Staff, Rod, Claw (2-Handed), Whip, Book

    -Weapon Damage types: Slashing, Piercing, Crushing, Melee (for fist-weapons), Fire, Ice, Lightning, Earth, Water, Wind, Light, Dark, Slime, Vermin, Dragon, Daemon, Humanoid, Ethereal, Undead

    -All weapons can inflict status ailments. There are many status ailments such as poison (of varying degrees), attribute lowering effects, stun, paralysis, sleep, mute, elemental degen effects, death, and so many more.

    -Weapons can imbue the wielder with special statuses as well, including an increase in attributes, magical barriers, increased speed, and much more.

    -Weapons can protect against any of the damage types or status ailments, so it is possible to come up with a defensive weapon.

    -Weapons can have special animations associated with them, anything from the conventional slashing animation to a fiery explosion or cascade of ice.

    -Weapons can have various additional non-conventional attributes attached, such as being able to attack twice, doubling the amount of damage absorbed when guarding, ensuring a first attack, and so forth.

    -Weapons can grant skills when equipped. For example, a wand of Chain Lightning can give the Chain Lightning spell to anyone who is holding it. Once they switch to another weapon, though, the spell will be gone.

    -Weapons can have special properties when its wielder reaches critical HP levels (25% health). This means you can have a generally benign weapon until its wielder is near death and then the weapon can unleash hell.

    -There currently can not be any ‘ranged’ weapons, such as bows, slings, x-bows and so forth. This -might- change in the future, but I doubt it.

    *Armor:
    -Armor types: Cloth, Leather, Ring/Chainmail, Platemail, Rings, Necklaces/Capes/Mantles

    -All armor can protect against any of the above mentioned damage types and status ailments. Aside from general defense and magic resistance, damage reduction is percentage based. For example: If a character puts on a helmet with 10% ice damage protection, it will reduce all incoming ice damage by 10%. This goes for status ailments, too. If aforementioned helmet also provides 15% poison resistance, then there is a 15% less chance of being inflicted by any level of poison.

    -Like weapons, all armor can imbue the player with special statuses and attributes. They can also grant non-traditional attributes, such as double attack, increased XP, increased gold gained in combat, and so forth.

    -Similarly to weapons, armor can grant skills and spells to the wearer. A magic ring can give its wearer a heal spell, for example. But that spell will be lost when the ring is taken off.

    -Armor can be relatively benign until the wearer reaches critical levels of HP, and then the armor’s true nature can be revealed.

    -Armor can enhance any attribute, just like weapons, so it is possible to have armor increase attack power, hit rate, critical strike rate, and so forth.

    *Items:
    -Items can do pretty much anything. They can increase attributes temporarily or permanently. They can give beneficial or negative status effects in or out of combat. They can cause damage or healing to characters or monsters.

    -Items don’t have to be combat-only. You can make an item that lets you see ghosts, or reveal hidden treasure. Other items can be used to decipher cryptic text, or grant invisibility to avoid monsters altogether.

    -Items can be potions, gems, scrolls, tools, instruments, food stuffs, ingredients, resources, or pretty much anything else that is not a weapon or piece of armor.

    -Items might be restricted based on art resources. Ask if a certain type of item is possible.

    *NPCs:
    -NPCs can be male or female with any age range, from child to elder.

    -NPCs can be non-human, but restrictions apply. Ask if you want to know if a certain race type is possible.

    -You can choose what weapons/armor your NPC are equipped with if any. You can also choose what item/s are dropped if the NPC is slain.

    -You can choose what the NPC looks like overall, but art resources might limit some choices. This will be judged on a case-by-case basis.

    Boss Monsters:
    -You can choose what the Boss Monster looks like, but art resources might limit the choices. This will be judged case-by-case.

    -You can choose what type of ‘normal’ attack the boss uses (it can inflict any of the above mentioned damage types with normal blows).

    -You can choose what resistance the boss has towards various elements and status afflictions. All bosses are immune to ‘death’ so that they are not instantly killable. You can also choose what elements or status afflictions the boss is weak against. All bosses must have some sort of strength and weakness.

    -You can choose what skills and spells the boss can use. If the boss has a particular elemental affinity, make sure the spells and skills are congruent. For example, if the boss is heavily fire-based, he should be using fire spells and skills.

    -You can choose just how strong the boss is by allocating the boss’ attribute points. You can choose the boss’ basic strategy.

    -You can create a more intricate boss strategy, including specific skills and spells to be used at specific times or under specific conditions.

    For creating anything, if you have any questions or are uncertain, just ask and I will do my best to answer your questions.

  • Will players be able to save their game anywhere or only at certain points?

    When the player is in the 'overworld' area of the game (that is, anywhere that is not a dungeon or similar area), then the player can save at any time. Inside of dungeons and similar areas, saving can only be done when the player comes across a special journal. Alternatively, the player can craft or purchase special journals which allows them to save anywhere inside of a dungeon.

    Last updated: Thu, Jun 23 2011 1:49 pm EDT
  • How do characters become stronger?

    Every character gains experience points (save for Mercenaries; they never grow stronger), and when enough are accumulated, the character in question levels up. Upon gaining a new level, the character receives a number of attribute points which can be put into a variety of stats at their liesure.

    Players also receive skill/spell points from leveling up (and also from defeating enemies and completing puzzles and the like) which are spent on learning new spells and skills. Each character has a bevy of abilities to choose from, and it is up to you to choose which order to unlock them in. There are hundreds of skills and spells to unlock. Some of the most powerful spells, though, will need to be assembled from ancient and long-lost manuscripts scattered throughout the land.

    Another large factor in what makes a character's stats is their weapons and armor. All weapons raise one attribute or another (and some lower certain attributes, like agility if it is a heavy weapon), and each type of weapon deals different damage. A mace will deal crushing damage while a sword will deal slashing damage. Different monsters are more resistent or susceptible to certain types of damage. Magic weapons might be enchanted with elemental damage or inflict status effects upon contact. Armor similarly raises a variety of attributes, primarily defense and magic resistence. Each piece of armor also offers protection against specific elements and status effects.

    There are a few other mechanics that will determine how powerful your characters become, such as the Guild that they join (if any), as well as their moral choices throughout the game.

  • What are the requirements to run Aleph?

    Although the game is not complete, I can say with confidence that Aleph will run on a large variety of PCs, including older ones. The prospective minimum requirements are as thus:

    Microsoft(R) Windows(R) 2000 / XP / Vista / 7
    1.0 GHz Intel(R) Pentium(R) III equivalent or higher processor
    256MB of system RAM
    1024x768 or better video resolution

    The amount of hard drive space is yet to be determined. It will probably be around 100MB.

    Last updated: Mon, Jun 6 2011 10:42 pm EDT
  • How many hours of gameplay can be expected?

    This answer is going to vary greatly depending on each individual player. If a player knows where everything is, and how to get everywhere and do everything required to beat the game, and does not bother with exploration, then the game will last 5-10 hours. For the average player's first-time-through with a moderate amount of exploration and side events and what not, I would say closer to 40-50 hours. For people who like to search every nook and cranny and find all the easter eggs and such, 70+ hours could easily be expected.

    Last updated: Wed, Jun 15 2011 1:57 pm EDT
  • Can this game run on Mac?

    The game itself cannot run on Mac, as it is designed to run on Windows-based machines. However, with a Windows emulator, the game should work. However, I do not have a Mac and cannot test this. If somebody does have a Mac with an emulator and would like to test this, send me a message!

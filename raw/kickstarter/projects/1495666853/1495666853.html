Why did the chicken cross the road? You'll find out why it's no laughing matter in this addicting arcade inspired video game.

8/27/2013 - It has become increasingly obvious that I've underestimated what it takes to launch a successful Kickstarter project. We got several things wrong, the biggest of which is to have enough supporters to drive traffic to our campaign and give us some momentum. The second biggest was our inability to get published on gaming websites because we weren't far enough along to have a gameplay video or prototype available.

We're not cancelling Nightmare on Chicken Street, but we are cancelling the Kickstarter campaign. It'll take a while--since we failed to get the funds needed to support us--but we'll eventually be back with an improved campaign.

If you are interested in receiving updates about our game, please sign up for our Newsletter, like us on Facebook, and/or follow us on Twitter.

"People often have dreams where they are falling from great heights. There is an urban legend that says a person will die in their sleep if they fail to wake before they hit the ground."

"There is a similar story in the chicken world. However, their nightmares are plagued with crossing roads..."

That premise is what sets the stage for the game. You take control of a dreaming chicken crossing an endless "nightmare world" highway. This highway consists of several thematically varied zones that are populated by a variety of vehicles, creatures, and other obstacles. This lengthy sequence eventually repeats, with some variance, and with everything receiving a slight boost in speed to increase the difficulty. The speed will be capped before it becomes impossible to control, which results in it becoming a test of skill and endurance.

The goal, and only way that your chicken will survive the night, is to get a new score in the Hall of Fame. While there are plenty of goals to keep completionists (like us) happy, Nightmare on Chicken Street wasn't designed as a game you can simply beat. It was inspired by classic arcade games, so it's easy to get into and meant to keep you coming back for more. It was designed to offer an impressive amount of personality and quality replay value in an extremely solid and well-polished package.

There will be five uniquely challenging single player modes with record lists and three competitive multiplayer modes that support up to four players (may include AI players, see Stretch Goals). The game also features an online hall of fame, a few dozen achievements, detailed statistics, a fun and challenging twist to your typical unlockable art gallery, and a nice in-game instruction manual.

As for the graphics, audio, and controls: The game features highly animated 2D graphics designed for 1080p screens, but also scales correctly to other aspect ratios without letterboxing. We plan on getting the best custom orchestrated music and sound effects that we possibly can. It can be played with a keyboard or Xbox 360 gamepad, and we hope to add generic gamepads and/or touchscreen controls as well.

A Few Characters:

Here are some chibi versions of the chicken and four of the enemies. From left to right: A Gooper, Roxy, Bo, Louie, and the Chicken that you play as. Goopers are blobs with mouths, eyes, and arms. They tend to group together into large deadly masses. Roxy, Bo, and Louie are ogres that inhabit the nightmare world. They are typically seen driving their respective vehicles (Scooter, Motorcycle, Roadster). The manual will contain more information about them and the other enemies in the game.

Become a Beta Tester!!!

Anyone that chooses the "Beta Tester" reward tier or higher will get the opportunity to participate in an early beta test. Throughout this period we'll be gathering feedback, suggestions, and bug reports in order to make us, you, and anyone that purchases the game as happy as possible with the final product. Testers will also be invited to participate in various polls, some of which will be based on feedback and suggestions that we've received.

You'll also have the chance to become a credited tester in our in game manual/credits. I have no idea how many backers we'll get, but space will likely be limited to those that clearly participated in the beta test.

"BoJo's Bazaar: Independent Video Game & Art Studio" is a business created by and consisting of me (Joe) and my brother Bobby. We currently sell artwork on various products through Zazzle and offer free desktop and mobile wallpapers. Nightmare on Chicken Street will be our very first video game.

BoJo's Bazaar is also part of a fictional world starring our mascot, BoJo the Elephant. This world and its characters will eventually be featured throughout our website and be the focus of some of our games, product designs, wallpapers, and hopefully a series of free web comics.

The minimum goal of BoJo's Bazaar is to allow the two of us to have a fulfilling career doing what we've always wanted to do: design and create video games that we can be proud of. Beyond that, we are determined to become a financially stable and highly respected game studio. Video games are our passion and we want that passion to show through with each and every game that we release.

If you want to know more about us and BoJo's Bazaar, just ask. Feel free to follow me on Twitter and Facebook as well.

It has taken us years of hard work to get to where we are. I've gone from having zero knowledge of programming and web design to being proficient in C#, PHP, HTML+CSS, and a little Java. I've also become much better at my primary interests: game design and writing. Bobby has gone from drawing as a hobby and having no experience with digital art to being excellent at both. He's also become very good at his primary interests: creature, character, and game design.

We've put our lives on hold for the last several years, both socially and financially, to prepare for and pursue this dream. Bobby has very little income, my work is just enough to pay the bills, and our website (which we created to help fund our games) barely pays for itself. Unfortunately, this process has taken longer than I hoped, and the plan of growing BoJo's Bazaar until it was capable of supporting us and funding our game development hasn't panned out.

Funding wasn't an option when we started, but with the advent and success of Kickstarter, us needing to hire someone to create the quality music and sound effects that we desperately want our game to have, needing some hardware and software to help complete the development, and needing to work full-time (or greater) for a year in order to achieve our design goals, we decided it might be just what we need to get us back on track.

Any help you can lend in realizing our dream would be greatly appreciated, whether it's backing this project, rallying us up some extra supporters, and/or giving us helpful advice. Our marketing/social media skills are limited and we lack a strong social circle (fans/friends/family) that can help us out, so the second option would be especially valuable.

We (mostly) finished the intro and title screen so that we could have some actual footage to show off with our Kickstarter project. There's still a lot of artwork and gameplay elements that need to be finished before actual gameplay footage will be ready. Most of the menus are functional, but there is still a lot of work left till they are finished (mostly the instruction manual, achievements, and stats).

Additionally, I'll most likely be switching our game engine to the Unity engine, which will take some time to learn and port all of my existing code to. The new engine will have superior support, features, performance, and allow us to release it on more platforms/marketplaces. Our current engine (and XNA) seems to be EOL and suffers from a severe stutter with multiple hardware configurations.

We plan on releasing the initial PC version sometime in the fall of 2014, but that date is not set in stone. We'd rather delay the game a few months than release a version that is buggy or doesn't live up to our design goals.

Nightmare on Chicken Street is being targeted for Windows/Linux PC's (Standalone/Steam/Windows Store). Mac and Windows Phone 8 might be possible, but we lack the hardware to test it on. Touch controls passing our quality standards is also something I can't guarantee.

We'd love to support additional platforms (especially the Xbox 360, PS3, and WiiU) and non-English languages, but it depends on how well it sells, the market potential, and the effort/time/costs involved in the ports. We do have stretch goals that cover this.

The goal we are hoping to achieve is $29,500.

This is enough to support us for a year, pay for the music and sound effects, buy some software that we need, and let us make some PC upgrades. This will allow us to work 100% full-time on the game and should last long enough to complete every version (standalone, Microsoft Store, Steam, and Linux) and all post release work.

Below is a list which breaks down the funding total.

  • Funding Fees: $2,360 to $2,950+
    Kickstarter and Amazon charge a combined 8-10% fee.
  • Reward Fulfillment: $4,150+
    An obvious and costly promise that needs to be fulfilled. Sadly, our game isn't digital reward friendly like an mmo, etc, so we had to include physical rewards and the cost to create and ship them.
  • Music and Sound Effects: ~$4,250
    This should allow us to get some very good music and sound effects created for our game.
  • Bills/Survival/Pay: $7800x2 (spread out over 12 months)
    $3.75/hour ($150/week) with no paid overtime probably doesn't sound like much, but we're frugal enough that it will allow us to work on the game full-time. This will stabilize our lives and improve our overall mental well-being (less financial stress).
  • Computer Software & Hardware: ~$2,550
    Windows 8 Pro ($200x1), Unity Pro ($1,500), and probably one or two Unity Extensions. I'd also like to get Bobby a more powerful CPU, more RAM (w/ECC), and each of us a nice reliable SSD dedicated solely to our work files.

Anything not spent or in excess of our goal will be put to very good use. We have many designs and ideas that are beyond what the two of us can create in a reasonable amount of time. The more we have, the faster we can achieve our goals. Here is a list of things that we would do if we had the funding or revenue to do so.

  • Upgrade our website from a shared hosting account.
  • Purchase development software like Photoshop and Visual Studio.
  • Have an actual marketing budget to work with.
  • Get a "Wacom Cintiq 24HD Touch" for Bobby to work on.
  • Get a monitor calibrator to give our art more accurate colors.
  • Move to a place where we can have an actual "office" to work in.
  • Begin expanding our team so that we can speed up game development.
  • Legal fees necessary to getting our business completely in order.
  • Additional months of Bills/Survival/Pay. Add health insurance if possible.
  • Port our games to consoles/handhelds/other devices.
  • Translate our website and games into different languages.
  • Partially fund our next game so we can actually have footage ready.
  • A good video camera/webcam/mic for videos.
  • A 3D printer to make figurines, ornaments, keychains, etc. ourselves.

Any additional funding would be highly appreciated. Here is a list of stretch goals that, if reached, will give us the funds to further improve our game, rewards, business, and chance of success. These additions will take extra time, so expect a delay proportionate to each addition.

Note #1: Our Art Gallery images will take a lot of time to create. These are high resolution desktop wallpaper quality images that will eventually be released as free wallpapers on our website. These wallpapers will not be available upon release, but will instead be released over time and after a few months have passed. This is so early players will get more enjoyment from unlocking them (less likely to be "spoiled"). Additionally, more pictures means there is more fun to be had unlocking them, plus the Art Gallery itself is a mini-game. The extra hardware budget will allow us to get a monitor calibrator. The raises are simply to make things less tight on us.

Note #2: We'll spend a few extra months improving the multiplayer by adding AI players/bots (I'm not an AI expert, so this will take a while) and improving the in game manual. This new manual will be more detailed and include more illustrations. Any extra time will be spent improving existing special effects and/or implementing new special effects. This should also give us enough to cover any legal fees for getting our business completely in order and pay for some marketing (includes having a trailer created for us).

Note #3: Xbox 360 Development kits are supposed to be pretty expensive. I can't seem to find an official price, but most sources say it is a whopping $10k/yr (plus a long process that involves a bunch of paperwork). Additionally, it's supposed to cost quite a bit for Unity console ports (only price I can find is WiiWare, which is supposed to cost $15k). If accurate, this extra funding is essential for an XBLA version. This goal should also take care of any legal fees, marketing, and any unforeseen costs associated with an XBLA release (see Risks and Challenges).

Note #4: We'll get Nightmare on Chicken Street on as many worthwhile Unity Engine supported platforms as we can. Unity for Android and iOS cost $1.5k a piece, plus we don't have any iOS or Android devices to test it. WiiU and PS3 are probably as expensive as the Xbox 360 (~$25k). Too early to tell with the Xbox One and PS4, but we'll definitely try to support those as well. We could also use a WP8 phone, a Windows 8 Tablet, and a Mac for testing. Any of the budget not spent on porting will be used to translate the game into different languages.

Note #5: This goal also gives us a raise to $7.35, the minimum wage in Missouri. I don't see us paying ourselves anything higher than this for the foreseeable future, any excess through funding and sales will be used to make our vision of BoJo's Bazaar become a reality. This will allow us to safely get a more suitable place to live/work (until we can sustainably pay for and fill an actual separate office) and is a must considering how difficult the reward stocking/shipping would be from our cramped apartment.

Physical items will likely experience some form of delay versus the digital items. Reaching our stretch goals and the extra work those entail will cause the rewards to be delayed along with the game itself. We'll keep you as up-to-date as we possibly can.

Hard copies, if the stretch goal is reached, will be created a while after the digital copies are released. This is simply to make sure the version on the physical copy is as bug free and final as possible. I'd already be pretty upset with myself if I missed a bug on the digital release, I guarantee I'd be more upset than anyone if I actually shipped a physical product with said bug.

A "Helper Monkey" is a character type in the fictional BoJo's Bazaar world. They are Captain Poppy Squawk's crew.

Risks and challenges Learn about accountability on Kickstarter

I honestly can't foresee any risk to anyone willing to fund us. We made plenty sure not to design the game outside of what just the two of us were capable of accomplishing. Nothing, except some freak tragedy, will keep us from finishing and releasing Nightmare on Chicken Street. There's always a chance it won't be released on time, but late or not, the game will definitely be released.

While I have the utmost confidence it will be accepted, it's ultimately up to Microsoft and Valve whether our game actually makes it onto their Marketplaces. The console marketplaces depend on whether the respective companies allow it as well, and we'll do everything we can to make it happen.

Our biggest challenges will be the final tweaking of everything until we are happy, finding the right person to do the music and sound effects, and marketing the game successfully. The rest is just work as usual.

Video games in school: the new way to engage students! Help us develop a skill-building, writing-based MMORPG for the classroom!

The Garden Online is a Massive Multiplayer Online Role Playing Game (MMORPG) for the classroom. That's right, an MMORPG... a video game. Why a video game? Video games do something very critical to the education of their players, and they do it so well that students don't even realize it’s happening: video games engage their players in real-world challenges by making the player believe they are within the role of the character. To reference a few recent titles, Call of Duty allows a student to define themselves as a military Special Forces soldier; Guild Wars 2 creates an environment where a student becomes a Sylvari Ranger fighting for the Dream; and Resident Evil 6 makes players believe they are Chris Redfield fighting to save the world from the zombie plague. While these games are each teaching our students concepts that will often not be used outside the story of the game, their method for doing so is very effective. ​

Notice that in none of my examples did I use the phrases “pretended to be,” or “acted as though.” This is because video games don’t simply ask a player to pretend to be the characters, or act as though they are committing the actions. If you ask a player what their goal is, they will often reply with statements such as “trying to defeat the bad guys” or “trying not to die.” These kinds of statements are exactly what The Garden Online focuses on: video games create a virtual environment where players are committed to being the character that they control. 

In a classroom that could use this method, players wouldn't simply pretend to be a chemist, writer, or mathematician; students would learn inside a virtual environment that mimics that of a real-world job atmosphere. In The Garden Online, students take on the role of a writing  career instead of pretending to practice within the traditional classroom. Risks are diligently calculated for the progression of the student; students become dedicated to an identity that they create, control, and develop as they learn; and students actually become a chemist, writer, or mathematician in a virtual environment instead of simply pretending to take on these roles in a classroom.

In short, The Garden Online simultaneously develops a virtual environment that encourages a student to take on the identity of a real writer with real goals while also allowing students to genuinely develop their writing skills in an authentic, skills-based environment, instead of just pretending in a classroom.  

As mentioned above, The Garden Online is a Massive Multiplayer Online Role Playing Game (MMORPG) modeled after such titles as World of Warcraft, Guild Wars, and Final Fantasy. These are the types of games that are prominent in the media, and those are the types of games that our kids are playing. There are other games called First-Person Shooter games, but because of the nature of those types of games, the MMORPG format was deemed to be more appropriate for a school environment.

The Garden Online will be developed using the Eclipse Origins graphics engine. This engine is written in Visual Basic 6, and though this language and style of gaming might be a bit dated, the kind of equipment available within the classroom is limited in regards to running a gaming software. Originally, the game was going to be developed using a low-resource 3D engine, but because the computers in the classroom are unable to run those games efficiently, we decided to lower the graphics intensity down to a 2D structure.

While the game does not graphically resemble games such as those popular titles mentioned above, I have included stretch goals (provided below) to possibly purchase hardware capable of running a more modern version of The Garden Online, and thus make this project even more successful.

The Story

The Garden Online’s primary goal is to teach English Language Arts students the foundational skills required to succeed in school, state testing, and their careers. The first step to understanding how this works is to understand the virtual world of The Garden. 

Many years in the future, the United States has been destroyed after a series of nuclear attacks left the land barren. In a last ditch effort to survive, citizens flocked to the nearest structure that might provide safety from the oncoming apocalypse. Finding refuge in government safe-havens, two colonies (the north and the south) of survivors have endured the nuclear winter that quickly followed the blasts. After a period of time, the colony from the north, made up of mostly industrial workers, rose up out of the ground to find their country ravaged and lifeless. Scavenging for edible food, potable water, and usable resources, the Industry - as they began to call themselves - discovered a land that somehow survived the destruction. As the members of the colony rejoiced, they began to settle upon this land and thrive from its resources.

A month passed, and after no evidence of human life was found, the Industry began to build a new world – one based on industrial work and the founding principles that once made the country great. One day, however, a small group of survivors appeared on the horizon and were welcomed into the Industry with open arms. The group spoke of other survivors living in a series of underground bunkers from the south. As more survivors began to appear, the leader of the Industry realized that his new-found resources would not sustain both colonies. In his desperation, he began turning away new survivors. This eventually led the southern survivors – the Harvest, as they began to call themselves – to the point of desperation.  In an attempt to force themselves into the settlement, the Harvest survivors began setting fire to the structures in a call for justice. This caused the Industry to cast out most residents whom were once part of the Harvest, and began to segregate those whom were allowed to stay. The segregation eventually led to a series of attacks from Harvest rebels. As the attacks grew worse, the Industry was forced to move their civilian settlements back to an area outside The Garden, leaving only militia to guard the resources.

Players here enter the game in a frantic struggle for control of the resources within The Garden. Players choose to be enlisted into the army of The Harvest or The Industry. However, as a frail society, each member must take part in the work required to maintain social order and strive for peace. Players then choose a professional career by becoming a scientist, politician, or journalist who attempts to persuade their respective citizens in their findings and opinions. By providing this service to their colony, they are rewarded with an income to help them buy the necessities that are vital to living in a crumbling society.

How The Garden Online Teaches Writing

This is where writing education comes in! Students choose a profession, much like they will after high school (and as an English Teacher, my hopes are that everyone will become a writer, of course...), and in The Garden Online, that profession is one to persuade the people  of the colonies on issues of peace and prosperity. Once the player chooses a profession (we’ll use the politician as an example), that profession is assigned quests, tasks, and objectives that increase in difficulty with each level’s progression. At level one, a player will work on understanding the basics of sentence structure, level two: creating a sentence, level three: identifying the parts of their own sentence, and so on. Each level will be progressively more complex, and as the players grow in both level and skill, certain levels will serve as a test for a series of previous levels. That is, at level five, the Politician “Trainer” as he’ll be labeled in the game (a name for the computer controlled character that guides the player in their professional career) will give the player an assessment over the previous four levels.

These tests and skill building exercises will not just be a “go to your trainer, write a few sentences, and you’re done” scenario, however. When the player first meets with the Politician Trainer, the trainer will send the player on a quest that will be pertinent to the story line – perhaps going to visit with the Harvest leader to discover his stance on certain political bills or laws. When the player goes on these quests, he will be thrust into a story line where the writing will matter - persuasion will be his tool and a chance for peace will be his goal. Of course, no matter which profession you choose (politician, scientist, or journalist), the goal will be to develop peace between the colonies; the politician will focus on relations via politics, the scientist will emphasize the importance of scientific research for both colonies, and the journalist will enlighten the people on important events, just like they would in real-life. 

The Garden Online uses the virtual environment to instill a feeling of identity and purpose within the player, and through this identity, gives the player real experience with becoming a professional in a real career, even though the career is set during the fall of the United States. As students play, they will grow from learning sentence structure to writing a full-blown persuasive essay. These essays will not be focused on a pretend situation in the classroom; instead, the essays will have real consequences within the world of The Garden Online, and in order to help create peace between the colonies, players will have to become a real writer with real risks and real rewards.

Aside from having the opportunity to pilot this program at your school if you become a backer at a certain level, the program will be based at Desert Winds High School in the Casa Grande Union High School District.  Desert Winds is a Title 1, self-paced alternative school for students whom have been referred due to discipline problems or lack of progress. When a student enters Desert Winds, they have entered into their last opportunity for success in high school, and if they don't succeed here, they are removed from the school to either pursue a G.E.D. or try again at the start of the next semester. 

What is a Title 1 school? The Title 1 description means that a majority of the students come from a low-income background, and more than 40% of the students receive a free or reduced fee lunch. As such, a lot of these kids are struggling to learn and succeed, and that just isn't happening in a traditional classroom. 

We need an alternative form of education for the alternative student, and that's exactly what The Garden Online provides.

Desert Winds has certainly gotten a bad reputation for being a place where the troubled and "bad" kids go, but having taught there for a number of years, I can assure you that the students at Desert Winds have received this label unfairly. I have taught at preparatory schools, charter schools, public schools, and private schools, but I have never had an easier and more fulfilling job than working at Desert Winds. The students here are trying their hardest to succeed and make their families proud. Students here are still stressed out over tests, nervous about prom, and heartbroken by failure on state testing. The only difference here is that students are on their last chance. As I said above, once students are removed from Desert Winds, they have to wait until the next semester to re-enroll, and this often does not happen. 

When a student is removed from Desert Winds, they feel as though they have failed, and often do not return to achieve their high school diploma. This is something that we cannot allow to happen; we need to provide an education that helps these kids succeed, and in order to do that, we must provide an alternative form of education that appeals to their interests and passions. This is where The Garden Online comes into play - an educational video game to appeal to a generation that loves video games.

As teachers, our job isn't to lecture and spout off information with the hopes that something will stick. Rather, our job is to present information in a meaningful and authentic way as to provide real experience that will prepare our youth for a higher education and a career. We can't continue presenting a classroom that promotes a traditional classroom environment - the traditional student no longer exists, and we have to find a common interest with these students if we want them to become engaged, invested, and dedicated to learning.

Earlier this year I was asked to pilot a new up-and-coming course funded by Title 1 entitled 21st Century English Language Arts. The course had no curriculum, no specific goal, and no course outline. The "21st Century English" (as it became known) was an initiative to integrate technology into every facet of learning within the classroom. We were given a SMART Board, an e-reader and laptop for every student, a sound system to help those student who have hearing troubles, and various other technologies to help reach students in their own culture - one that communicates through text message, touch screens, and smart phones. 

As part of this class, I want to integrate a writing-based video game that can parallel the benefits of programs such as Read-180, Edgenuity, and Odyssyware. These programs (especially Read180) give a heavy emphasis to reading education, and engage students with software technology that is interesting and engaging. The problem, however, is that educational writing software is almost non-existent, and students have very few programs that appeal to them in the same way that educational reading software does. 

The Garden Online will provide the avenue through which students can become engaged with writing software on the computer. Instead of answering questions that hold no personal meaning, students can engage in a story-line in which they play a vital role. Everything they choose to do will affect their outcome, and their writing skills will grow as they become more engrossed in the world of The Garden Online. The best part? The kids will be having fun doing what they love while learning the skills that they need for success after high school.

The Garden Online is aligned to the up-and-coming Common Core State Standards. When constructing the quests, tasks, and goals of each professional class (job), the Common Core standards are the guiding light. Before writing out the mechanics of the professional class (job) careers, the Common Core standards are broken down into parts that can be practiced and assessed as pieces that can be clearly observed. For example, the first writing standard for grades 9-10 (labeled CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.9-10.1a)  states that students should be able to "Introduce precise claim(s), distinguish the claim(s) from alternate or opposing claims, and create an organization that establishes clear relationships among claim(s), counterclaims, reasons, and evidence." As clear and specific as this standard might seem (I say this with tongue firmly planted in cheek), there are several parts to this standard that must be tended to. The student would start with first identifying what a claim is, and then move on to identifying claims within certain reading samples.

How will this be done in the world of The Garden Online? After a student chooses their profession (suppose they choose scientist), they will then meet with their trainer to begin working on professional quests that focus on writing skills. The quest that practices the Common Core standard mentioned above would require a player to travel to the town governor and speak with him about the claims of his colony. The story would appear as text being spoken by the governor, and as the student reads, the governor will ask questions to lead the student to understanding what the claim is, and then to identify the claim. Once the claim is identified, the player would then leave the governor's home and return back to their trainer. The trainer will then ask a series of questions in which the player will be required to respond correctly. By the time the quest is completed, the student would have finished his first lesson in identifying claims, as required by Common Core State Standard CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.9-10.1a. 

Of course, now the player would begin to build on top of this skill, and work on creating their own claims based on encounters and conversations with other NPC's (NPC's are computer controlled characters). Once this skill has been mastered over the course of a level or two (each level will require several quests to gain enough experience to move on to the next level), the player will then begin writing their own claims based on further conversations, events, and experiences in the game. While these events and conversations will be controlled by the game, their will be so many quest choices that a player will have the opportunity to choose their own learning track based on which quests they pursue. 

That doesn't mean that a student can get away with doing only those standards which are easy for them. On the contrary, each level will only offer quests that pertain to a particular standard (for example, all quests in level four will only focus on a particular standard). The students, however, will have the choice of which quest they choose, and how they go about practicing a particular standard during a specific level. To make this a little more clear, imagine a student who is working on level four and doesn't want to travel to the governor to find out what claims he has to offer about the opposing colony. The player can instead go to the local market gossiper and find out what claims she is making about the local barber. Though the student may be able to skip certain quests, standards will not be allowed to be skipped - this allows students to move quickly through the levels in which they are strong and also gives them options when working on their weaknesses.

The 21st Century English course that I currently teach is set up to track the individual progress of each and every student. Every one of my students has their own individual portfolio that only he and I have access to. Inside this portfolio, I track the students progress on state testing, homework scores, vocabulary, journaling, and a wide spectrum of other activities that I regard as significant to the student's success as a writer and student. At the beginning of the year, I give the student assessments to see where they are at the beginning of the year, and then I reassess using a similar assessment (an assessment with different questions, but the same testing objectives to ensure students don't grow too familiar with the tests). Based on these assessments, surveys regarding the game, and assignments particular to writing level, I assess a student's growth from the point they enter the class to the point they reach their individually-based growth goal and graduate the course. 

As the semester continues, I will monitor each students individual growth, their time spent playing The Garden Online, and the time spent taking part in other classroom activities. This study will also be integrated into my own dissertation as I research the effects of multiplayer gaming on education.

Risks and challenges Learn about accountability on Kickstarter

All materials are currently in stock, and we see no setbacks in our production timeline. As the designs and development ideas are already completed, the timeline in which we plan to complete The Garden Online is based solely on programming and modifications. For this reason, there is no reason as to why the production of the game would be pushed back.

The goal of The Garden Online is not to make a profit, and the audience is not the commercial gaming population. Rather, The Garden Online is intended to increase the learning opportunity and academic success for those struggling students in Desert Winds High School. Because the game has no intentions of being a commercial game for profit, once funding is received, The Garden Online will be produced as far as described above; the funding requested is all that is needed to complete the game, and therefore, once received, the game will be completed.

Dear... person reading this,

My name is David Klingler. I started Solanimus in 2011, and have released two games so far: Cool-B in Search of Floyd, and Crashland. Cool-B in Search of Floyd was a very personal project for me, as it was about cats I had in real life, and also because I did everything on the project by myself. When the game came out, it was very polar in how people viewed it. People either loved it, or really didn't like it at all. The most dividing thing was the graphics, which were meant to be child-like, but were often just seen as not good. Still, the amount of fan mail I've received for the game has shown me that a lot of people really like the game, so I feel that it was a success for that reason and also because I learned so much while creating it.

For my second game, Crashland, I had my dad do the graphics. He's an artist professionally, and he made it look great. Crashland is still being updated with lots of new features.

The reason I've gone into indie game development is because I see so much potential in games as a medium, and because the game industry is what is relevant to me. I think games can become so much more than they are right now, and I want to be a part of that furthering of the medium. I'm not just a game developer, but also a game historian, game collector, game reseller, competitive gamer, and... a Scottish fiddler.Â 

Since starting Solanimus in 2011, I've been supporting everything by myself mainly by doing reselling of vintage games. I have been trying hard to expand Solanimus, and now have Paul working part-time, my dad working part-time, and an intern, Daniel, working part-time. I don't have enough money yet to get Paul full-time, and that's partly what this kickstarter is for. Starting a game company is hard enough with money, and it's considerably harder without money. Supporting a game company still in start-up with game reselling doesn't leave much room for anything, so I've turned to kickstarter.

Paul needs to go full-time because he is currently working another job to support himself and that unfortunately takes up most of his time. He still tries to do as much as he can for Solanimus, but it's simply impossible for him to work full-time on this game at this point without more funding. Paul wants nothing more than to work on Signal to Noise full-time, and he has the passion, skillset, and ability to adapt that make him an excellent game developer. He's played a big part in the game so far, but in order to finish it, we need him to go full-time Solanimus.

Some games that have influenced Signal to Noise:

  • Tempest
  • Starfox 64
  • Rez
  • Typhoon 2001


So the thought came like this:

How can we make a music game that's just as intense as the most intense music? The more related the gameplay is to the music, the deeper the connection. The more intense the music is therefore would make the gameplay more intense, and the more intense the gameplay is would make the music seem more intense. We need to make a game that's based around the music that's playing, and let people use whatever song they want, to play an awesome game that REALLY has a connection to the music. We could make a bullet-hell type game. No, let's make it a 3D rail shooter, that would be awesome.

Signal to Noise is a music-driven rail shooter. We're first releasing on desktop platforms, but we hope to be able to make a version for mobile devices as well, and maybe even consoles. You can choose any song from your own personal library, and play a visually intensive rail shooter with the gameplay and visuals being generated in real-time off of the music you chose. You can customize the game to play how you want to play, and always have a unique experience with each song you own.

Going along with our philosophy that games can be much more as a medium, we feel that the increased connection between gameplay and sound can give players feelings that normally wouldn't be possible in a game. Also, the customization options and ability to use any song make the game infinite in permutations.

While the demo only features one mode of gameplay on one song, the game is being made to allow players to customize all of the game mechanics to play the type of game that they want to play at all times. There is a way to have racing (yes, with multiplayer), all shooting and dodging, escape sequences, boss battles, an episodic campaign, and much more, and every detail of the gameplay can be customized. The gameplay elements having to do with the music are of course also being worked on so that the level is even more involved with the song playing.

In Signal to Noise, you are piloting a craft that's actually going through spacetime in an act of quantum teleportation. Quantum teleportation is the transmission of atoms to another location at the speed of light. In order for a quantum teleport to work, there must be a strong signal and always have a good Signal to Noise ratio, just like the sound coming from your speakers when listening to music. If the Signal to Noise ratio in your speakers is too bad, you won't hear the music, so if the Signal to Noise ratio is bad in the beam of light during an act of quantum teleportation, the teleportation will fail.

Perhaps in the future we'll travel between stars using quantum teleportation. When doing so, the Signal must be managed at all times throughout the entire time of travel. This is the gameplay of Signal to Noise. You must constantly keep the Signal going and keep the Noise controlled. Players can do this using permutations of rules and settings along with any song of their choice. The game is then generated based off the song, and the player jumps into the Signal. So why is the game based off of music if you're traveling in a beam of light? That's a quantum physics question; for now, we'll leave that to your imagination.

The funds from this kickstarter will specifically be used for the following things:

  • Paul going full-time: He must be financially supported to a minimum, and we need this funding in order for that minimum to be reached.
  • Unity Pro licensing: We're using Unity for this project, but we need Unity Pro licenses in order to make the best game we can. Unity Pro gives us the ability to use certain features of the Unity engine that will make the game much better including post-processing effects.
  • Other Necessary software and assets: There are still some extra things we need to purchase to make the game the best it can be, and we need this funding to do so.
  • Paying the office rent: We want to make the best game we can, so I can't be worrying about making money for office rent while working all the time on the game.



It's a Group Effort

If you have any ideas that you would like to see in the game options, please let us know! If you pledge enough to get beta access, we can work with you during development to add features that you want.

If you're a musical artist, please feel free to contact us if you'd like for your music to be promoted through Signal to Noise.

Risks and challenges Learn about accountability on Kickstarter

If Signal to Noise is successfully funded, we will obviously have noticeably fewer risks, but there are still some. Any game's development can end up being delayed, so we might actually not be able to send out the rewards by February, but we'll try. We're progressing with the game's development, but there's still a lot that needs to be done and it's simply not possible to predict how long it will take to finish with much accuracy. The quality of the game is much more important to us than releasing quickly.

Plenty of unexpected things are expected (?) to come our way during the remainder of the development of Signal to Noise, but game development and the trials it brings is nothing new for us. We know how to get past adversity and complete a game, and can confidently say that the game will be finished no matter what.

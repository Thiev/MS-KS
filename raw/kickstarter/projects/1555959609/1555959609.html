Simply to the Point

Read just the first two paragraphs here and watch the video above if you want the simple pitch. Click the link here or above to see a detailed, but fairly quick and to the point pitch.

Match domipop pieces > [R][B] in sets of 3 or more as they're tossed into the main field. The name Domipop comes from combining dominoes (since they come in pairs and can be stacked exactly like dominoes) and pop, (the action cleared pieces take as they're wiped from the field). Matching colors compatible colors (red, blue and purple) will also cause a piece to be cleared and will allow for higher points to be scored along with more sophisticated chains. The music is never dull and keeps you interested (See Update #3) while playing Domipop. Domipop is more than just this single, core gameplay mode. The adventurous variations and challenges of the many other game modes (See Updates #2 and #6) will be more than enough to keep you entertained for dozens, if not hundreds of hours.Â

The Game

Puzzle games are fun, simple and a great way to sneak a bit of enjoyment in life while waiting for something mundane to happen. Like waiting for a license renewal or for a lecture to end. Even better is a game that steals your time and attention in the same manner that a significant other does. By being as beautiful as it can and you not being able to do a thing about it nor wanting to either.

Tentatively named Domipop, this puzzle game combines many elements that are comparable to successful elements from other games in the genre but it sets off on its' own path by itself in many ways at once. Pieces fall into the play area like messengers are tossed into Sparta's pits. The pieces that fall are simple pieces divided into two parts like a Puyo, or more relevantly a domino. Each half shines like a Bejeweled jewel but is more akin to a piece from Columns. Bonuses are given for clearing lines like Tetris, but the objective of the game is to clear away gems as efficiently as possible.

The colors can be matched and made to disappear in sets of 3 or more. That type of gameplay is simple to allow anyone to jump into the action. The twist, the part that truly makes Domipop come into its' own is the importance of the colors of the domipops (domipop pieces). They are red, blue, yellow, purple, orange, green, and white/clear. 3 or more red pieces that touch horizontally, vertically or diagonally will be wiped from the field. When they do so, the pieces have gravity and will cause all matched pieces to fall into the background accordingly.Â 

Any matches resulting from that rearrangement will also be wiped from the board, creating a chain or more appropriately a â€œdomino effectâ€ when being cleared from the board. The more skillful players, or in some cases the more serendipitous a chain of events are, the higher the score and stronger the "effects" in the game. There are also special bonuses for clearing pieces from wall to wall. Straight Chains occur when you clear pieces from edge to edge in one move all at once. Flush chains occur when pieces are cleared from a chain that clears a piece in every column, it will be possible to chain Flush Chains.

Falling or place-able pieces can be set in the same way that a domino can be set. Either horizontally or vertically. They mimic dominoes in another way also, the pieces do not align in whole number increments on a grid like a normal puzzle game. Instead, they can align in half (0.5 unit) increments. Imagine a red-blue piece sitting horizontally across the top of a purple domipop but with both colors touching the purple piece at the same time (like a â€œTâ€). The three pieces will clear. Red and blue together make purple, there will be a physical animation that will show the pieces mixing together to show that they'll match.

There will be an option/mode that turns off automatic color combining/switching and allow for it to be done manually (or not at all) but in the core game it is automatic. The clear/white (undecided) domipop is a bridge piece. Meaning that it will change color but the current plan is to allow it to do so when both a touching piece is being wiped off of the field and the change will allow for another wipe. The clear/white piece is too complicated right now and may be simplified.

What I've described to you so far is just the core of the gameplay and the basis for every gameplay mode. I will describe the rest of the currently planned modes in future updates and both can and will elaborate further on anything if you would please send a question. I'm open to answering anything relevant to the project and maybe a few things that aren't, but at my discretion of course. I have multiple single player modes that touch upon the basic modes of this genre, but will also innovate by using staples like Ghosts. Ghosts are sharable data that allows for competition against another person based upon a saved version of their plays, that along with some inspired ideas from other genres along with a few more twists and creations of my own will make this one of the most entertaining puzzle games that you have ever played.

Multiplayer will also be a blast and will be filled with diversity. It will encompass many different ways to play from including a mode that is similar to, but doesn't ape the Push mode from Tetris DS to a using Android OS based equipment as controllers/connected opponents for the PC version of the game. Enough about the actual gameplay and gameplay ideas... for now. I will now tell you about the plans for the platforms distribution if you'll be kind enough to read it.

The Hardware Targets

For personal computers Windows XP and above will be supported, with a stretch goal of also supporting Windows CE (yes, everyone with the $100 CVS netbook running Windows CE will have another fun game to play!). I will also be aiming for Linux using a 2.6 or above kernel and a gui will be supported (may possibly change to support 2.4 or earlier along with being more specific with the required utilities). Native BSD support is a stretch goal, but will not be a part of this projects initial goal. I do promise to test the linux client with the BSD compatibility layer until I'm able to build directly for it. I should be able to do so without any problems but I don't want to promise official BSD support via a BSD client yet.

I personally dual boot Windows 7 64 bit, and a very custom (whose Arch system isn't?) Â Arch Linux 64 bit installation. Made the switch from Linux Mint (I love that distro) a few months ago. I will test the linux build on 32 and 64 bit versions of Ubuntu, Fedora, Mandriva (or Mageia), Arch and OpenSuse. I will be very open about when I'm testing on my website, if you have a distro that you would like for me to test that isn't based upon any listed here, please let me know and I'll add it to the test list. Just know that for immediate development purposes, those five are the only distributions that I will be testing on. I'm a linux supporter, but if I tested for every distribution I would never make any progress :P. LSB, what is that?

Moving on to portable devices, Android OS (2.1 and above with 1.6 being a possibility), WebOS and OpenPandora will be supported. Android is growing at an awesome pace, WebOS may have a future with Amazon but even if it doesn't it is a beautiful OS with a few users that should be hungry to join in on the action right now. OpenPandora is a great homebrew, open source device that is finally getting into the hands of users. It has a small base of users, but they usually own other devices besides an OpenPandora. It will be great to support for gaining more potential coverage of the game plus it's just a wonderful device. The size of a DS but very potent. Outside of a Xperia Play, it's the next major Handheld device supported here that has controls similar to that of a handheld gaming system. It offers a lot of potential equally to both developers and users. It's a very exciting device, even if it did show up to the party fairly late (but it was fashionably late).Â 

Platform Breakdown: Windows XP and all later versions, Linux 32 and 64 bit, Android OS (2.1+), WebOS (TouchPad is the main focus here), and OpenPandora. Yes, I noticed that Mac OS X and iOS were left out. I do not dislike the platforms, but they are very expensive to develop for right now. I do not own a single Apple device, heck I don't even use iTunes. The cost of a Mac, iPad and iPod Touch along with developer fees for the iOS and Mac App Markets would greatly increase the amount of funding needed to get this program off of the ground. I've already registered with Android Market, Amazon App Market, Kongregate, Intel AppUP, WebOS app store and have plans of eventually approaching Steam, Desura and a few other digital stores. If the game is popular enough to support me and allow for the purchasing of Mac hardware then I will happily support the platform.

Apple platforms do have users that spend more than Android users and many other platform's users. The cost of entry is fairly high and I would like to get the game into as many potential markets and peoples hands as possible. The combination of many other Markets and platforms that may be ignored will help in increasing my games visibility. Instead of just being another puzzle game (despite the innovations in gameplay that players will care about), it will be the game that allows for many families or people to play together regardless of the platform. I strongly believe in what I have planned here. Unfortunately the Apple platforms may have to wait to receive the game.

Funding Goals, Purpose, and a Promise

Notice that I said may. If funding reaches 200% then I WILL support those platforms as soon as I receive the funds and purchase all of the needed hardware. If you would like to donate Mac/iPod Touch/iPhone/iPad to me, also be my guest. I'll gladly appreciate you for it. The testing does more than just test the games core feature of that platform but also interoperability between and within the same platform. Basically, can a Samsung galaxy S II, Xoom, LG Optimus V, TouchPad, WinVista and Ubuntu all play together on a networked game? You don't have to answer that question, I do. I can set the network code up "right" but I need to test it for latency and other issues between devices. The Vista user shouldn't lose because they're a second behind everyone else. They should lose because they're getting beat and beat badly.

The money that is being raised is to cover buying the hardware that I need to test the game on, like a TouchPad and Palm Pre, A middle of the road Android tablet (Maybe a Nook Color?) and a few older pcs and hardware to change its' configuration to test it on many pieces of hardware. I know that I'm making a simple puzzle game here, not Duke Nukem Eternity but bugs and other glitches usually show up in weird places and setups. Not only that but I need to pay for the licenses needed so that I may be able to develop and distribute the game legally. I would also like to buy the domain name and copyright to the real name of Domipop to help protect the brand.Â 

I've already promised Apple support if funding reaches 200%. I'll open source the game under a major open source license that is closer to copylefting than copyrighting if the fund reaches 300% of its' goal. Do know that the available source will be behind by about 2-3 versions due to testing purposes and me not wanting to release dirty code. The available source will always be the source for every version of the game that is available commercially, not for testing. If the game sells well enough in all of its' combined markets to support me solely on its' profits (not having to work a 'real job') then I will also open the source under the same conditions outlined above. It's something that I plan to do eventually, just thought that I'd share the conditions that I once kept private for it.

I like the open source community, I don't believe that everything needs to be or should be open source but it would be nice if it was. I'm more of an advocate for open standards and for releasing software that isn't beta software. That's not to discount anyone else's work, but I will not release something with a known bug that hinders the quality of the game. It may become a â€œfeatureâ€ (famous nerd joke) if it's cool but you won't get constant crashes unless your device really is that quirky. Even then, I'm working with you to resolve the issue. If I get to a point where I'm opening the source based upon sales, do know that Xbox 360 via Xbox Live Indie and Windows Phone 7 versions will not be too far behind.

The Passion Behind the Plan

If you haven't noticed, I target a lot of platforms with a low barrier to entry. I was born in 1987 and while growing up on an NES, Genesis and GameBoy I could only dream of having the type of opportunities that are so easily available today. It's no longer a dream to want to work for Nintendo or to go beyond and choose to compete with them and everyone else. It's very possible. Copying may be considered the sincerest form of flattery but I love a challenge. I'm sincerely flattered when people compete with me or believe that they are or choose me as a point to surpass. I may not be on their radars but I'll be competing with all of the developers that helped to shape my childhood and I don't believe that there's any other career in the world that could make happier than that.

The main tool that I use for development is GLBasic for programming. It's a very versatile tool and allows for C code to be implemented within its' own IDE and executed smoothly. It's able to compile to many platforms with the same code (a bit of tweaking for controls, resolution, etc. are still needed). Not every version of the game will be exactly the same due to certain platforms strengths and control methods. I will allow for as much control customization as is reasonable to include but I will design the default control schemes to fit like a glove for standard play. Thank you for taking the time to read this, please continue to check back in the future for updates.
